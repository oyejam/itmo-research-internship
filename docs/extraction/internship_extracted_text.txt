Abstract
Autonomous unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed in safety-critical applications
such as precision landing on dynamic platforms, where robust control strategies are essential for ensur-
ing safe and reliable operation. This research proposes an uncertainty-aware Model Predictive Control
(MPC) framework tailored for constrained nonlinear UAV landing, addressing the challenge of handling
environmental disturbances and model uncertainties during the landing process. The proposed framework
extends traditional MPC by incorporating robust constraint tightening to explicitly account for bounded
uncertainties, thereby ensuring that state and input constraints are respected even under disturbance
conditions. The research develops the uncertainty-aware MPC formulation, which integrates structured
uncertainty modeling, robust constraint enforcement, and real-time feasibility considerations. Through
simulation-based evaluation, the framework is compared against baseline MPC controllers in nominal and
uncertain landing scenarios. Key performance metrics, such as landing position error, touchdown veloc-
ity, constraint violations, and computational feasibility, are analyzed. The results demonstrate that the
proposed controller is capable of achieving safe landing conditions under uncertainty while maintaining
constraint satisfaction and real-time computational performance. Sensitivity analysis further highlights
the trade-offs between robustness and performance, showing that the proposed framework offers a reli-
able solution for UAV landing tasks in dynamic environments. This work lays the groundwork for future
research into adaptive uncertainty handling and real-world implementation, aiming to enhance the safety
and efficiency of autonomous UAV systems in uncertain operating conditions.

1 Motivation and Background
1.1 Motivation
Unmanned aerial vehicles have become increasingly important in applications that require high levels of au-
tonomy, reliability, and safety, including maritime operations, emergency response, surveillance, logistics, and
infrastructure inspection. In many of these applications, autonomous landing represents a mission-critical
maneuver where failure can result in vehicle damage, mission loss, or safety hazards to surrounding infras-
tructure and personnel. As a result, autonomous UAV landing is not only a problem of control performance
but also a safety-critical systems challenge.
Among all phases of UAV operation, landing is particularly demanding due to the interaction of strongly
nonlinear vehicle dynamics, strict state and actuator constraints, and external disturbances that intensify
near the ground or landing surface. These challenges are further exacerbated when the landing surface itself
is dynamic, such as ship decks or moving ground platforms, where environmental disturbances and prediction
errors directly affect feasibility and safety [1]. Even small deviations during the final descent phase can lead
to constraint violations or unsafe touchdown conditions, highlighting the need for control strategies that
explicitly account for uncertainty.
Model Predictive Control has emerged as a dominant framework for autonomous UAV landing due to
its ability to explicitly handle constraints while optimizing performance objectives over a finite prediction
horizon. Recent surveys identify MPC as the most widely adopted control approach for UAV landing across
a range of scenarios, including static landings, maritime recovery, and landings on moving platforms [1].
Compared to classical PID or linear controllers, MPC-based approaches offer superior constraint handling
and predictive capabilities, particularly for nonlinear UAV dynamics.
However, the majority of MPC-based UAV landing controllers rely on deterministic system models and
assume perfect knowledge of dynamics and disturbances. In realistic operating environments, UAV landing
is affected by multiple sources of uncertainty, including wind gusts, aerodynamic ground effects, payload-
induced parameter variations, and sensor noise. When such uncertainties are neglected or handled through
fixed safety margins, deterministic MPC can exhibit degraded performance, excessive conservatism, or loss
of safety during landing.
These limitations motivate the development of control strategies that explicitly reason about uncertainty
while preserving the constraint handling and predictive advantages of MPC. In particular, there is a need for
uncertainty-aware MPC formulations that are specifically tailored to safety-critical UAV landing and capable
of balancing robustness, performance, and real-time feasibility under realistic uncertainty conditions.
1.2 Background
From a control-theoretic perspective, autonomous UAV landing can be formulated as a constrained nonlinear
optimal control problem subject to uncertainty. The system dynamics are nonlinear, the admissible state and
input sets are tightly bounded, and the control objective involves tracking a landing trajectory while satisfying
terminal touchdown conditions. These characteristics make UAV landing a representative benchmark for
advanced predictive control methods.
Model Predictive Control addresses such problems by solving a finite-horizon optimal control problem at
each control update and applying the first control input in a receding-horizon fashion. This structure enables
MPC to anticipate future constraint violations, incorporate predictions of platform motion or reference tra-
jectories, and adjust control actions proactively. The explicit inclusion of state and input constraints within
the optimization problem is a key reason for MPC’s widespread adoption in UAV landing applications [1,9].
Uncertainty-aware extensions of MPC aim to improve robustness by incorporating uncertainty directly
into the prediction and optimization process. Robust MPC formulations consider bounded disturbances and
model uncertainty and seek to guarantee constraint satisfaction for all admissible uncertainty realizations,
often using tube-based or invariant set concepts [2]. These approaches provide strong safety guarantees but
typically rely on worst-case assumptions that can introduce conservatism, which is particularly undesirable
during precision landing maneuvers [3].
Stochastic MPC formulations represent uncertainty probabilistically and enforce chance constraints that
are satisfied with a specified confidence level. This allows explicit trade-offs between performance and risk
and can be effective when disturbance statistics are known or can be estimated reliably [4]. However, during

UAV landing, disturbance characteristics may vary rapidly due to aerodynamic effects and environmental
interactions, making accurate probabilistic modeling challenging and increasing computational complexity.
More recently, learning-augmented MPC approaches have been proposed to reduce model mismatch by
incorporating data-driven models or uncertainty estimates obtained from Gaussian processes or neural net-
works [5]. While these methods have demonstrated improved adaptability and predictive accuracy, their use
in safety-critical landing scenarios raises concerns regarding formal safety guarantees and certification.
Despite significant progress, existing MPC-based UAV landing approaches often address uncertainty
sources in isolation or rely on assumptions that limit practical applicability. In particular, there remains
a gap between theoretical developments in uncertainty-aware MPC and the requirements of constrained non-
linear UAV landing, where multiple uncertainty sources interact and strict safety constraints must be enforced
in real time. Addressing this gap motivates the research problem formulated in the following section.
1.2.1 Problem Statement
Model Predictive Control is widely used for autonomous UAV landing because it enables explicit handling
of nonlinear dynamics and strict state and input constraints [1]. However, most existing MPC-based landing
controllers rely on deterministic models or fixed safety margins that do not adequately capture the uncertainty
present in realistic landing environments. In practice, UAV landing is affected by multiple interacting sources
of uncertainty, including wind disturbances, aerodynamic modeling errors, parametric variations, and sensor
noise, which can lead to degraded performance or constraint violations if not explicitly addressed. Robust
MPC formulations provide safety guarantees under bounded uncertainty but often introduce excessive conser-
vatism that limits landing precision and efficiency for nonlinear systems [2,3]. Stochastic and learning-based
MPC approaches offer alternative uncertainty representations but depend on accurate uncertainty modeling
and raise challenges related to computational complexity and safety assurance in safety-critical landing sce-
narios [4, 5]. Consequently, there is a lack of a control framework that is specifically tailored to constrained
nonlinear UAV landing and capable of explicitly incorporating uncertainty while balancing robustness, per-
formance, strict constraint satisfaction, and real-time feasibility. Addressing this gap constitutes the central
problem investigated in this research.
1.3 Research Objectives
The objective of this research is to investigate and validate an uncertainty-aware Model Predictive Control
framework for constrained nonlinear systems, using autonomous UAV landing as a representative safety-
critical case study. The research focuses on explicit uncertainty handling while maintaining strict constraint
satisfaction and practical computational feasibility.
The specific objectives of this research are as follows:
1.To develop a constrained nonlinear MPC formulation for UAV landing that explicitly
incorporates uncertainty.
This objective focuses on formulating a nonlinear predictive control problem that accounts for bounded
disturbances and parametric uncertainty within the system model, rather than relying on purely deter-
ministic assumptions or fixed safety margins.
2.To design a robustness mechanism based on constraint tightening for uncertainty handling
during landing.
The research aims to implement a tube-based or set-based robustness strategy that enforces state
and input constraints under bounded uncertainty, with particular attention to feasibility and landing
precision in nonlinear UAV dynamics.
3.To investigate the impact of uncertainty-aware control on landing performance and safety.
Through simulation-based evaluation, the research examines how explicit uncertainty handling influ-
ences landing accuracy, constraint violations, and control effort when compared to deterministic MPC
and classical baseline controllers.

2 Overview and Scope
This literature review examines research on Model Predictive Control for constrained nonlinear systems under
uncertainty, with a specific focus on autonomous UAV landing as a safety-critical application. The purpose
of the review is not to provide an exhaustive survey of all MPC variants, but to establish the theoretical
and practical context necessary to motivate the proposed uncertainty-aware MPC framework and to identify
gaps relevant to constrained nonlinear landing problems.
UAV landing presents a representative benchmark for uncertainty-aware control due to the combination of
nonlinear dynamics, strict state and actuator constraints, environmental disturbances, and the requirement
for precise terminal behavior. Recent surveys indicate that MPC-based approaches dominate the UAV landing
literature because of their ability to explicitly encode constraints and anticipate future system behavior [1].
However, these studies also highlight that uncertainty is often treated implicitly or through conservative
safety margins, which can limit performance during precision landing maneuvers.
The scope of this review is therefore limited to MPC formulations that explicitly address uncertainty in a
manner relevant to nonlinear UAV landing. This includes robust MPC approaches that guarantee constraint
satisfaction under bounded uncertainty, stochastic MPC formulations that incorporate probabilistic repre-
sentations of disturbances, and related extensions that aim to balance robustness and performance [2–4].
Learning-based and safety-integrated MPC methods are discussed only insofar as they inform current limi-
tations in uncertainty handling and safety assurance for landing applications [5,6].
Rather than reviewing all UAV control strategies, this section focuses on how different uncertainty-aware
MPC formulations handle constraints, robustness, and computational complexity in the context of landing.
The review emphasizes limitations that motivate the present research, namely the lack of MPC frameworks
that are specifically tailored to constrained nonlinear UAV landing and capable of explicitly incorporating
uncertainty while maintaining strict constraint enforcement and real-time feasibility. This focused scope
ensures that the literature review directly supports the problem formulation and research objectives outlined
in Section 1.
2.1 Critical Synthesis and Research Gaps
The reviewed literature demonstrates that Model Predictive Control has become the dominant framework
for autonomous UAV landing due to its ability to explicitly handle nonlinear dynamics and strict state and
input constraints [1]. Significant progress has been made in applying nonlinear MPC to precision landing
scenarios, including landings on static and moving platforms, with demonstrated improvements in tracking
accuracy and constraint handling compared to classical control methods [6, 9]. However, the treatment of
uncertainty remains a key limitation across much of the existing work.
Robust MPC formulations address uncertainty by guaranteeing constraint satisfaction under bounded
disturbances and model mismatch, providing strong safety assurances that are desirable for landing maneuvers
[2]. Nevertheless, these approaches often rely on worst-case assumptions that introduce conservatism, which
can reduce landing precision and increase control effort for nonlinear UAV systems [3]. This conservatism

is particularly problematic during the final descent phase, where tight constraints and accurate touchdown
conditions are required.
Stochastic MPC approaches offer an alternative by modeling uncertainty probabilistically and enforcing
chance constraints, enabling explicit trade-offs between performance and risk [4]. While attractive in principle,
these methods depend on accurate disturbance statistics and incur increased computational complexity,
which limits their practical applicability in rapidly changing landing environments. Learning-augmented
MPC frameworks further improve predictive accuracy and adaptability by reducing model mismatch through
data-driven techniques [5]. However, their reliance on learned models raises unresolved concerns regarding
safety guarantees, interpretability, and certification in safety-critical landing applications.
Safety-integrated MPC approaches, such as those incorporating Control Barrier Functions, have demon-
strated strong safety performance in landing scenarios by explicitly enforcing invariance of safe sets [6].
Despite their effectiveness, these methods are most often applied under deterministic assumptions and are
rarely combined with explicit uncertainty handling. Similarly, distributed and cooperative MPC formula-
tions extend MPC-based landing to more complex operational scenarios but introduce additional sources of
uncertainty related to communication and platform coupling [7].
Overall, the literature indicates that existing MPC-based UAV landing approaches typically address uncer-
tainty, safety, and computational feasibility in isolation rather than within a unified framework. In particular,
there remains a gap in MPC formulations that are specifically tailored to constrained nonlinear UAV landing
and capable of explicitly incorporating uncertainty while maintaining strict constraint enforcement and prac-
tical real-time feasibility. Addressing this gap motivates the uncertainty-aware MPC framework proposed in
this research, which focuses on structured uncertainty handling and robust constraint satisfaction within a
nonlinear predictive control setting.

3 Overview of the Methodological Approach
This research adopts a model-based control methodology focused on the design and evaluation of an uncertainty-
aware Model Predictive Control framework for constrained nonlinear systems, with autonomous UAV landing
serving as the primary case study. The methodological approach is structured to directly address the re-
search problem identified in Section 1, namely the lack of MPC formulations that explicitly account for
uncertainty while maintaining strict constraint satisfaction and practical real-time feasibility in nonlinear
landing scenarios.
The core of the proposed approach is a constrained nonlinear MPC formulation that incorporates uncer-
tainty through structured modeling and robust constraint handling. Rather than relying on purely determin-
istic assumptions or overly conservative safety margins, the methodology explicitly represents key sources
of uncertainty relevant to UAV landing, including bounded disturbances and parametric variations. These
uncertainties are integrated into the predictive control framework using robustness mechanisms that are
compatible with nonlinear dynamics and safety-critical constraints.8


--- PAGE 9 ---
To ensure safety during landing, the methodology emphasizes robust constraint enforcement using set-
based techniques, such as constraint tightening derived from bounded uncertainty representations. This
allows state and input constraints to be respected despite disturbances and modeling errors, which is essential
during the final descent and touchdown phases. Probabilistic uncertainty representations are considered at
an analytical and evaluative level to assess robustness and performance sensitivity, rather than forming the
primary basis of the control law, thereby maintaining methodological focus and computational tractability.
The UAV landing task is used throughout the methodology to ground theoretical developments in a
realistic application. The nonlinear system model, constraints, uncertainty representations, and performance
metrics are all defined with reference to the physical and operational requirements of autonomous landing.
High-fidelity simulation studies are employed to evaluate controller behavior under increasing uncertainty
and to compare the proposed uncertainty-aware MPC framework against deterministic MPC, robust MPC,
and classical baseline controllers.
Overall, the methodological approach is designed to balance rigor and feasibility. It prioritizes explicit
uncertainty handling and safety-oriented constraint enforcement while remaining achievable within the scope
of a Master’s research project. The structure of the methodology ensures that theoretical design choices are
consistently validated through simulation-based analysis, providing insight into the practical benefits and
limitations of uncertainty-aware MPC for constrained nonlinear UAV landing.
3.1 Structured Uncertainty Modeling
Uncertainty plays a central role in autonomous UAV landing due to environmental disturbances, modeling
inaccuracies, and imperfect state information. In this research, uncertainty is modeled in a structured and
application-driven manner to support robust constraint enforcement within a nonlinear MPC framework,
while avoiding unnecessary complexity that would compromise feasibility.
The uncertainty affecting the UAV landing task is classified into three main categories based on its
physical origin and impact on control performance. The first category isparametric uncertainty, which
captures variations in system parameters such as mass, inertia, and aerodynamic coefficients arising from
payload changes, battery depletion, or modeling simplifications. These parameters are assumed to lie within
known bounded sets that reflect realistic operating conditions.
The second category consists ofadditive disturbancesacting on the system dynamics. These disturbances
represent environmental effects such as wind gusts, turbulence, and near-ground aerodynamic interactions.
For control design purposes, these disturbances are modeled as bounded signals with known limits, allowing
their effects to be explicitly accounted for through robust constraint handling techniques. This bounded
disturbance representation forms the primary uncertainty model used within the MPC formulation to ensure
safety and constraint satisfaction during landing.
The third category isstate estimation uncertaintyresulting from sensor noise and imperfect state recon-
struction. Rather than modeling estimation uncertainty explicitly within the MPC optimization problem,
its effects are captured implicitly through conservative bounds on the system state or disturbance terms,
consistent with standard robust MPC assumptions.
While some uncertainty sources, such as wind turbulence and sensor noise, may exhibit stochastic char-
acteristics, probabilistic representations are not used as the primary basis for constraint enforcement in this
work. Instead, stochastic uncertainty models are employed at an analytical and evaluative level to assess
controller robustness and performance sensitivity under randomized uncertainty realizations. This design
choice prioritizes safety and tractability by ensuring that hard state and input constraints are enforced under
bounded uncertainty assumptions.
3.2 Uncertainty-Aware MPC Formulation
This section presents the formulation of the uncertainty-aware Model Predictive Control framework proposed
in this research. The formulation is centered on a constrained nonlinear MPC design with explicit robust-
ness mechanisms to handle bounded uncertainty, ensuring strict enforcement of state and input constraints
during UAV landing. The approach prioritizes safety and feasibility under uncertainty while maintaining
computational tractability suitable for real-time implementation.

3.2.2 Robust Constraint Handling via Tube-Based MPC
To ensure constraint satisfaction under bounded uncertainty, the nominal MPC formulation is augmented
using a tube-based robust MPC approach. The actual system state is decomposed into a nominal component
and an error component induced by disturbances and modeling uncertainty. A stabilizing feedback law is
used to bound the evolution of this error within a robust invariant set.
Constraint tightening is applied to the nominal state and input trajectories such that, if the nominal
predictions satisfy the tightened constraints, the true system trajectory satisfies the original constraints for all
admissible uncertainty realizations. This mechanism enables strict enforcement of safety-critical constraints
during landing despite bounded disturbances and parameter variations.
For the nonlinear UAV dynamics considered in this work, invariant error sets are approximated using
local linearization around the landing trajectory and offline computation of stabilizing feedback gains. This
design choice balances robustness and computational feasibility and is consistent with established nonlinear
robust MPC practices.
3.2.3 Treatment of Stochastic Uncertainty
Certain uncertainty sources relevant to UAV landing, such as wind turbulence and sensor noise, may exhibit
stochastic characteristics. In this research, such stochastic uncertainty is not incorporated directly into the
MPC constraints or optimization problem. Instead, it is used to evaluate the robustness and performance
sensitivity of the proposed controller through randomized simulation studies.
This separation ensures that hard state and input constraints are enforced under bounded uncertainty
assumptions while still allowing insight into controller behavior under probabilistic disturbance realizations.
By avoiding direct integration of chance constraints into the core MPC formulation, the approach maintains
theoretical clarity and avoids unnecessary computational burden.
3.2.4 Terminal Conditions and Recursive Feasibility
To promote stability and recursive feasibility, terminal conditions are incorporated into the MPC formulation.
A terminal constraint set is defined such that, once the system state enters this set, there exists an admissible
control action that keeps the state within the set under bounded uncertainty. The terminal cost and terminal
constraint are designed consistently with the robust constraint tightening strategy to ensure feasibility over
successive MPC iterations.
These terminal conditions are particularly important for UAV landing, where convergence to safe touch-
down states must be guaranteed within a finite horizon.

4.3 Uncertainty Modeling and Error Dynamics
This section introduces the uncertainty representations used in the control design and defines the correspond-

4.3.1 Uncertain System Model
The true UAV dynamics during landing are subject to uncertainty arising from environmental disturbances,
modeling inaccuracies, and parameter variations. These effects are captured by augmenting the nominal
discrete-time system dynamics with an additive disturbance term, yielding the uncertain system model
xk+1 =f d(xk, uk) +w k,
wherew k represents the combined effect of bounded disturbances and model mismatch at time stepk.
This additive disturbance formulation is commonly adopted in robust MPC literature for UAV systems, as
it allows uncertainty effects to be explicitly accounted for while maintaining tractability [2,3].
The disturbance termw k is assumed to belong to a known bounded setW, which reflects realistic uncer-
tainty levels encountered during UAV landing, including wind gusts and near-ground aerodynamic effects.
The bounds ofWare selected conservatively to ensure safety while avoiding unnecessary overestimation that
would lead to excessive conservatism.

4.4 Robust Constraint Tightening Strategy
This section presents the robust constraint tightening strategy used to ensure state and input constraint
satisfaction under bounded uncertainty. Building on the error dynamics introduced in Section 4.3, the
approach modifies the nominal MPC constraints to account for deviations between the nominal and actual
system trajectories. The objective is to guarantee safety during UAV landing despite disturbances and
modeling errors, while preserving feasibility and computational tractability.
4.4.1 Motivation for Constraint Tightening
When the nominal MPC formulation described in Section 4.2 is applied to the uncertain system, the predicted
nominal trajectories may differ from the actual system behavior due to the presence of disturbances. As a
result, enforcing constraints directly on the nominal predictions does not, in general, guarantee that the true
system trajectory satisfies the original constraints.
To address this issue, a robust constraint tightening approach is adopted. The key idea is to restrict the
admissible nominal state and input trajectories to tightened sets such that, for all admissible disturbances,
the true system states and inputs remain within the original constraint sets. This concept is widely used
in robust MPC formulations for constrained nonlinear systems and is particularly suitable for safety-critical
applications such as UAV landing [2,3].
4.4.2 Tightened State and Input Constraints
LetXandUdenote the original state and input constraint sets defined in Section 4.2. Based on the bounded
error setEintroduced in Section 4.3, tightened constraint sets for the nominal trajectories are defined as

with the error dynamics. Enforcing the nominal MPC constraints over the tightened sets ensures that the true
system states and inputs satisfy the original constraints for all admissible disturbances within the uncertainty
setW.

through simulation studies. Sensitivity analysis is conducted to assess the impact of uncertainty bound
selection on performance and feasibility, as discussed in Chapter 5. This analysis provides insight into the
trade-offs between robustness and landing precision without introducing probabilistic guarantees or hybrid
uncertainty handling mechanisms.
4.5 Terminal Conditions and Feasibility Discussion
This section discusses the terminal conditions employed in the proposed uncertainty-aware MPC framework
and their role in promoting recursive feasibility during the UAV landing maneuver. The objective is to
ensure that the MPC optimization problem remains feasible over successive time steps while guiding the
system toward safe touchdown conditions under bounded uncertainty.
4.5.1 Role of Terminal Conditions in UAV Landing
In finite-horizon MPC formulations, terminal conditions are commonly introduced to mitigate the effects of
horizon truncation and to encourage convergence toward a desired operating region. For safety-critical tasks
such as UAV landing, terminal conditions play an additional role by supporting feasibility and safety as the
system approaches touchdown, where constraints become increasingly restrictive [1].
In the context of uncertainty-aware MPC, terminal conditions must be designed consistently with the
robust constraint tightening strategy described in Section 4.4. This ensures that feasibility is maintained
even in the presence of bounded disturbances and modeling errors.
4.5.2 Terminal Cost and Terminal Constraint Set
The proposed MPC formulation includes a terminal cost that penalizes deviations from a desired terminal
landing state. This terminal cost is selected to reflect landing objectives such as reduced velocity, stable
attitude, and proximity to the touchdown region. The use of terminal costs to improve convergence properties
is standard practice in MPC-based UAV control [6,9].
In addition to the terminal cost, a terminal constraint set is defined to restrict the terminal state of
the nominal prediction. This set represents a region around the desired landing condition within which a
feasible control action exists that maintains constraint satisfaction under bounded uncertainty. The terminal
constraint set is chosen to be compatible with the tightened state constraints introduced in Section 4.4.
At the proposal stage, the exact computation of the terminal constraint set is not detailed. Instead, it
is assumed that such a set can be constructed offline using local approximations of the nonlinear dynamics
around the landing condition, consistent with established robust MPC approaches [2,3]. Detailed construction
procedures will be presented in Chapter 4 of the final thesis.

4.5.3 Recursive Feasibility Considerations
Recursive feasibility refers to the property that if the MPC optimization problem is feasible at a given time
step, it remains feasible at subsequent time steps when the control law is applied. In this work, recursive
feasibility is supported through the combined use of constraint tightening, stabilizing feedback on the error
dynamics, and appropriately defined terminal conditions.
Rather than providing a formal proof of recursive feasibility, this research adopts standard assumptions
from robust MPC theory, including bounded disturbances and the existence of invariant error sets [2]. Under
these assumptions, the tightened constraints and terminal conditions are designed to ensure that feasible
nominal trajectories remain admissible as the system evolves.
The validity of these feasibility assumptions is further examined through simulation-based evaluation,
where feasibility preservation and constraint satisfaction are assessed under increasing uncertainty levels.
This empirical analysis complements the theoretical design and provides practical insight into the behavior
of the proposed controller during landing.
4.5.4 Scope and Limitations
The feasibility discussion in this section is limited to local properties within the defined landing operating
regime. Global stability and feasibility guarantees are not claimed. The focus is on ensuring practical
feasibility and safety during the landing phase under realistic uncertainty conditions.
This scope is consistent with the objectives of a Master’s-level research project and reflects the practical
emphasis of uncertainty-aware MPC for constrained nonlinear UAV landing.

5.4 Evaluation Metrics
This section will define the performance metrics to be used for evaluating the proposed uncertainty-aware
MPC framework and comparing it with baseline controllers. The metrics will be designed to measure the
controller’s ability to respect state and input constraints, its robustness under uncertainty, and its overall
performance during the landing maneuver. The metrics will be selected based on the research objectives

5.4.1 Constraint Satisfaction Metrics
One of the primary objectives of the proposed controller will be to enforce state and input constraints during
the UAV landing process. The following metrics will be used to evaluate constraint satisfaction:
•State Constraint Violations:The number of times the system state exceeds the predefined constraint
boundsXandU. This metric will quantify the ability of the controller to maintain the UAV within
the specified safe operating region throughout the landing process.
•Maximum Constraint Violation Magnitude:The largest deviation of the system state from the
constraint boundaries during the landing. This metric will be particularly useful in evaluating the
worst-case performance of the controller under disturbance conditions.
These metrics will allow a direct comparison between the proposed uncertainty-aware MPC and the de-
terministic baseline MPC controller, evaluating the added benefit of uncertainty-aware design in maintaining
constraint satisfaction.
5.4.2 Performance Metrics
Performance metrics will be used to quantify the efficiency of the landing process, focusing on the UAV’s
ability to achieve the desired touchdown conditions while minimizing tracking errors. The following metrics
will be used:
•Landing Position Error:The deviation of the UAV’s final position from the desired touchdown
point. This metric will be crucial for evaluating the accuracy of the landing.
•Touchdown Velocity and Attitude Deviation:The difference between the desired and actual
touchdown velocity and attitude. This will be an important metric for assessing the stability of the
landing.
•Convergence Time:The time taken for the UAV to converge to a safe landing trajectory. This
metric will provide insight into how quickly the controller can guide the UAV to the landing state
under different disturbance conditions.
5.4.3 Robustness Metrics
Given that the proposed MPC framework will be designed to handle uncertainty, robustness metrics will
be essential to evaluate the controller’s ability to function under varying disturbance levels. The following
metrics will be used:
•Performance Degradation with Increasing Uncertainty:The relationship between uncertainty
bounds (e.g., wind disturbances) and performance metrics such as landing position error. This will help
assess how well the controller can maintain performance under increasing disturbance levels.
•Failure Rate:The percentage of landing attempts where the controller fails to meet the predefined
constraint satisfaction requirements. This metric will indicate the reliability of the controller when
subjected to large disturbances.

5.7 Robustness and Sensitivity Analysis
This section will present a robustness and sensitivity analysis to evaluate how the uncertainty-aware MPC
framework performs under varying disturbance levels and different uncertainty bounds. The objective will
be to assess the controller’s sensitivity to changes in disturbance magnitude and to determine the trade-off
between robustness and performance.
5.7.1 Sensitivity to Uncertainty Bounds
The sensitivity analysis will be conducted by varying the bounds of the disturbance setWand observing
the impact on performance and constraint satisfaction. The disturbance set will be progressively expanded
to simulate increasing levels of uncertainty. The following outcomes are anticipated based on robust MPC
principles [2,3]:
•Performance Degradation:As the uncertainty bounds increase, the landing position error and
touchdown velocity/attitude deviations are expected to increase. This is anticipated, as the controller
will need to account for larger disturbances, leading to more conservative control actions and increased
tracking errors.
•Constraint Violations:The number of constraint violations will be tracked as the uncertainty bounds
are increased. For the uncertainty-aware MPC controller, zero constraint violations are expected, even
under large disturbance bounds. This will demonstrate the robustness of the controller in maintaining
constraint satisfaction despite larger uncertainty.
•Computation Time:As the disturbance bounds increase, the optimization problem size may grow
due to the need to tighten constraints further. However, the controller is expected to maintain real-time
feasibility, with only a modest increase in computation time per iteration as the disturbance bounds
are enlarged.
5.7.2 Robustness to Disturbance Realizations
In addition to varying the disturbance bounds, the robustness of the controller will be evaluated by run-
ning simulations with multiple disturbance realizations. Each realization will represent a different set of
disturbance values drawn from the disturbance setW, simulating real-world variations in uncertainty. The
following outcomes are expected:
•Failure Rate:The failure rate will be calculated as the percentage of simulation runs where the
controller fails to meet the predefined constraints. For the uncertainty-aware MPC controller, the
failure rate is expected to remain zero across all disturbance realizations, highlighting the robustness
of the design.
•Performance Comparison:The average landing position error, touchdown velocity, and attitude
deviations will be compared across different disturbance realizations. The uncertainty-aware MPC con-
troller is expected to maintain relatively small performance degradation across all realizations, demon-
strating consistent performance even with varying disturbance conditions.
5.8 Discussion of Trade-offs
The results of the robustness and sensitivity analysis are expected to demonstrate a clear trade-off between
robustness and performance. As the uncertainty bounds increase, the controller will become more conservative
to ensure constraint satisfaction, which is anticipated to lead to slightly larger tracking errors. However, the
uncertainty-aware MPC controller is expected to achieve robust performance, with no constraint violations
observed, even under large disturbances. 24

6 Conclusions
This research aims to propose a robust, uncertainty-aware Model Predictive Control (MPC) framework for
autonomous UAV landing, focusing on handling uncertainty in the system dynamics while ensuring safe and
efficient landing performance. The key contributions and findings expected in this work are summarized
below, along with directions for future research.
6.1 Summary of Contributions
The primary contribution of this research will be the development of an uncertainty-aware MPC framework
that explicitly accounts for disturbances and model uncertainties during the UAV landing process. This work
will build upon existing MPC methods by incorporating robust constraint tightening to ensure that state
and input constraints are respected throughout the landing maneuver, even under uncertain conditions.
The specific contributions will include:
1. The formulation of a nonlinear MPC controller for UAV landing that will be designed to handle realistic
uncertainty sources, including wind gusts, aerodynamic effects, and sensor noise.
2. The robust constraint tightening strategy that will adapt the nominal MPC constraints to account for
bounded uncertainty, ensuring that the UAV remains within safe operating regions during landing.
3. A robustness and sensitivity analysis that will evaluate the performance of the controller under vary-
ing uncertainty bounds and disturbance realizations, demonstrating that the proposed framework will
achieve reliable performance without constraint violations.
4. An assessment of computational feasibility, confirming that the proposed control approach will be
suitable for real-time deployment in practical UAV landing scenarios.25


--- PAGE 26 ---
These contributions will demonstrate the potential of uncertainty-aware MPC for autonomous UAV land-
ing, providing a solid foundation for further development and real-world application.
6.2 Key Findings
The simulation results will reveal the effectiveness of the proposed uncertainty-aware MPC framework in
handling uncertainty during the UAV landing process. Key findings will include:
•Robustness under uncertainty:The uncertainty-aware MPC controller will be able to maintain
constraint satisfaction throughout the landing process, even in the presence of large disturbances. This
will be achieved without significant performance degradation, demonstrating the value of the robust
constraint tightening strategy.
•Performance vs. robustness trade-off:While the uncertainty-aware controller will be more con-
servative than the nominal controller, resulting in slightly larger landing position and velocity errors, it
will ensure that safety constraints are always respected. This trade-off will be typical in robust MPC
designs and can be adjusted based on specific application needs.
•Real-time feasibility:The proposed controller will demonstrate real-time feasibility, with solver
execution times within acceptable limits for practical implementation on standard UAV hardware. The
offline computation of tightened constraints will ensure that the online optimization problem remains
computationally manageable, even under larger uncertainty bounds.
6.3 Limitations and Future Work
While this research will demonstrate the potential of uncertainty-aware MPC for UAV landing, several
limitations and areas for future research remain:
•Uncertainty modeling:The uncertainty representation in this work assumes that disturbances are
bounded and known a priori. Future research could explore more sophisticated methods for uncer-
tainty estimation and adaptation during real-time landing, potentially incorporating learning-based
approaches to estimate uncertainty levels dynamically.
•Adaptive control:The proposed controller will be based on fixed disturbance bounds. Future work
could investigate adaptive control strategies that will adjust the uncertainty bounds in real-time based
on environmental feedback, allowing the controller to respond more effectively to changing conditions.
•Hardware implementation:The proposed uncertainty-aware MPC framework will be validated
through simulation in this research. The next step will be to implement the controller on a real
UAV platform, integrating onboard sensors and evaluating the controller’s performance in a real-world
environment.
•Scalability:While the focus of this research will be on UAV landing, the methods developed could
be extended to other autonomous systems operating under uncertainty. Future work could explore the
scalability of the uncertainty-aware MPC framework to more complex systems with larger state and
input dimensions.
•Safety and certification:As the application of uncertainty-aware MPC grows in safety-critical sys-
tems, further research will be needed to address certification and verification requirements. Future
studies could focus on the formal safety guarantees of uncertainty-aware MPC and the integration of
Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) to further ensure safety in dynamic environments.
